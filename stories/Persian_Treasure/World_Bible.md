## World Overview

Persian Treasure takes place in a real, contemporary Khuzestan — a province defined by oil infrastructure, irrigation engineering, ancient ruins, and economic strain, not by exoticism or fantasy. Every location a writer sets a scene in must feel like a place that exists today, with its own bureaucracy, weather, and economic logic. The ancient caravan thread is a layer underneath this world, not a separate fantasy world layered on top of it. Writers should treat the modern and ancient layers as two true things happening in the same physical ground, 2,500 years apart, never as a portal between two different kinds of reality.

---

## Present-Day Setting

The story is set in the present day, in and around a mid-sized provincial town in Khuzestan and the unauthorized excavation site near it. The town has real infrastructure: a few paved roads, irrigation canals, a struggling local economy tied to agriculture and informal oil-sector work, spotty but present mobile connectivity, and small under-resourced municipal and provincial offices. Nothing in this setting should feel remote-from-civilization or untouched — it is a working, populated region with its own pressures, not a blank wilderness waiting to be discovered.

---

## Historical Layer

The historical layer is the Achaemenid Empire, centered on a caravan that vanished along a regional trade route around 474 BCE, during the reign of Artaxerxes I. This layer is experienced only through Daniel's impressions — it is never an independently confirmed flashback. Writers should render the historical layer with the same physical specificity as the present day: real caravan logistics (pack animals, supply needs, guards, trade goods), real seasonal and geographic constraints, and plausible administrative reality of the Achaemenid road and provincial system. Avoid generic "ancient Persia" imagery — ground every detail in what is specifically known or reasonably inferable about this period and region.

---

## Geography

The region sits on the alluvial plains of southwestern Iran, where modern industrial development, agricultural irrigation, and ancient settlement patterns overlap on the same flat, river-shaped terrain. Distances between locations should be realistic for a province this size: the excavation site, the town, Susa, and the industrial zones are all drivable within a single day, not separated by days of travel. Heat haze, flat horizons, and long sightlines across cultivated or fallow fields define the visual geography.

---

## Khuzestan

Khuzestan is a real Iranian province with a layered identity: agricultural heartland, oil-industry backbone of the country, and home to some of the oldest continuously significant settlements in human history. Writers should hold all three identities simultaneously — this is not a province defined only by antiquity, nor only by industry, but by both pressing against each other in the same landscape. Ethnic and cultural diversity in the region (including Arab Khuzestani communities) should be treated with the same grounded respect as any other real-world detail — present, specific, never reduced to set dressing.

---

## Karun River

The Karun is the major river defining the region's agriculture, irrigation, and historical settlement patterns. It is a working river — used for irrigation canals, crossed by aging infrastructure, occasionally flooding, occasionally low during drought years. The river should feel like infrastructure and lifeline simultaneously, not a scenic backdrop. Excavation and caravan-route scenes near the river should reflect real seasonal water behavior (high water in spring from snowmelt, lower and more exposed banks later in the year).

---

## Susa / Shush

Susa (modern Shush) is the nearby ancient Achaemenid capital and the clearest historical anchor point for the caravan's plausible route and purpose. The modern town of Shush coexists with its ruins — visitors, low-key tourism infrastructure, a site museum, and ongoing archaeological presence are all real and usable. Writers can use Shush as a source of legitimate historical/archaeological characters, records, and institutional friction (permits, research access, academic skepticism).

---

## Industrial Zones

Oil and petrochemical infrastructure are a visible, audible, and economically dominant presence throughout the region: pipelines, flare stacks, refinery lights on the horizon at night, heavy trucks on rural roads, and a local labor market shaped by the industry's hiring cycles. These zones should appear as background reality and occasional plot friction (restricted access, security checkpoints, environmental impact tensions) rather than as a separate "industrial" set piece disconnected from the rest of the world.

---

## Restricted Areas

Several categories of restricted access exist in this world and should be used consistently:
- Active oil/petrochemical company land, with private security and limited public access.
- Designated archaeological protection zones, where unauthorized digging is a legal offense.
- Informal restricted zones near unstable excavation pits or old wells, restricted for safety rather than law.
Writers should treat each category distinctly — the consequences and gatekeepers for trespassing on oil company land differ from those for unauthorized excavation near a heritage zone.

---

## Legal Reality

Iranian cultural heritage law treats unauthorized excavation and antiquities removal as a serious criminal matter, not a minor infraction. Daniel's excavation contract exists in a legally grey, unauthorized space from the outset — this should create persistent, realistic background pressure (risk of inspection, risk of report, risk of confiscation) rather than being resolved or forgotten after its introduction. Any character with legal or institutional authority (heritage officials, police, provincial administrators) should act with plausible bureaucratic caution, self-interest, and limited resources — not as cartoonish obstacles or saviors.

---

## Heritage Protection

Legitimate heritage protection in this world is under-resourced but real: understaffed local heritage offices, occasional inspections, academic researchers with more passion than funding, and a genuine cultural value placed on protecting sites like those around Susa. Writers should be able to draw on this for institutional characters who are sympathetic but constrained, not villainous — bureaucracy and limited resources, not malice, are the primary obstacle this institution presents.

---

## Black Market Antiquities

A real, low-visibility black market for antiquities exists in this world, operating through informal networks, middlemen, and opportunistic buyers rather than organized-crime spectacle. This should be usable as a quiet, ever-present temptation and risk for Daniel and other characters — a way to convert discovery into desperately needed money — without becoming an action-thriller subplot. Any black-market interaction should carry believable legal and personal risk consistent with the Legal Reality section above.

---

## Environmental Hazards

The physical environment is genuinely dangerous in mundane, real ways:
- Extreme heat and dehydration risk, especially during excavation work in summer months.
- Dust and poor air quality, particularly near industrial zones and during wind events.
- Scorpions and venomous snakes in and around excavation sites, ruins, and rural terrain.
- Unstable, partially collapsed wells and old excavation pits, posing real fall and cave-in risk.
- Structurally unsound ruins and unsupported tunnels/trenches at the excavation site.
These hazards should be treated as constant background risk factors usable across many scenes — not one-off action set pieces — reflecting Daniel's real professional concern as a structural/civil engineer.

---

## Technology and Investigation Tools

Daniel and other characters have access to plausible, present-day tools: basic surveying and structural-assessment equipment, mobile phones with inconsistent signal near the site, laptops for research and communication, possibly ground-penetrating survey equipment if narratively justified and historically/technically plausible for the budget level of this excavation. No technology in this world should exceed what a small, underfunded, unauthorized dig could realistically access — no advanced or speculative tech.

---

## Social Reality

The local community is shaped by real economic pressure: agricultural and oil-sector employment cycles, young people leaving for work elsewhere, a mix of generational ties to the land and frustration with limited opportunity. Local characters should reflect a believable range of attitudes toward outsiders, toward the excavation, and toward antiquities — curiosity, suspicion, indifference, or opportunism, depending on the character, never a uniform reaction.

---

## Economic Pressure

Financial precarity is the primary real-world engine driving the contemporary plot. This should be visible not just in Daniel's personal situation but in the world around him: crew members who need to be paid on time, local officials whose cooperation may be tied to compensation, financiers who view the excavation purely as a return on investment. Money should function as constant narrative pressure throughout the world, not only in scenes explicitly about Daniel's finances.

---

## Cultural Texture

Daily texture should include: tea culture, family and community obligation, religious practice as a normal background presence (not a plot device), regional Khuzestani Arabic and Persian language coexistence, and the rhythms of agricultural and industrial work. Writers should favor specific, lived-in details (what a character eats, how a meeting is conducted, what is considered polite or rude) over generic "Middle Eastern" set dressing.

---

## Visual Atmosphere

Flat, heat-hazed horizons; exposed earth and dust at the excavation site; the contrast between irrigated green agricultural land and dry fallow ground; industrial lights and flare stacks visible at night; modest, functional architecture in town; the site museum and ruins at Shush as a contrasting "preserved" space against the raw excavation pit. The ancient-layer impressions should be visually grounded in the same palette and lighting logic as the present day — never stylized as a separate "vision" aesthetic.

---

## Audio Atmosphere

Wind across open ground, distant industrial machinery and flare-stack hum, irrigation water movement, insects and birds at dawn/dusk, vehicle engines on unpaved roads, tools striking earth and stone at the excavation site, and the ambient quiet of rural Khuzestan at night broken only by industrial or animal sound. Avoid musical or sound-design cues that editorialize the ancient layer as "other-worldly."

---

## Smell / Heat / Dust / Sensory Palette

Heat that is physically oppressive and exhausting, not merely described as "hot." Dust that coats skin, clothing, and equipment, and irritates eyes and throat. The mineral smell of disturbed earth and old stone at the excavation site. The smell of diesel, oil, and machinery near industrial zones. Cool relief inside thick-walled buildings or near water, used sparingly as contrast. These sensory details should be used to ground Daniel's physical exhaustion, which is itself part of the ambiguity around his impressions.

---

## Rules of Reality

- All present-day institutions, hazards, and economic conditions behave as they would in the real world.
- No character in this world has confirmed supernatural ability, knowledge, or perception.
- All historical claims used in scenes must be consistent with the Khuzestan historical and geographical research backing this package.
- Physical danger (heat, structural collapse, wildlife) is always real and always has real stakes — never narratively "safe" by default.

---

## Rules of Ambiguity

- Every impression, vision, or "inherited memory" beat experienced by Daniel must have at least one sufficient mundane explanation available to the player at the time it occurs.
- No scene, character, ending, or piece of evidence may confirm that Daniel's impressions are genuinely supernatural in origin.
- No scene, character, ending, or piece of evidence may confirm with certainty that Daniel's impressions are purely psychological, if doing so would foreclose the inherited-memory interpretation entirely.
- The ambiguity must remain genuinely balanced across the full story, not tipped decisively toward either explanation by the end.

---

## What Writers Must Never Do

- Never write a scene that has a character, document, or event confirm the supernatural explanation as objectively true.
- Never write a scene that treats heritage law, environmental hazards, or financial pressure as inconsequential or easily bypassed.
- Never reduce Khuzestani culture, language, or religion to generic or exoticized set dressing.
- Never introduce technology, abilities, or resources inconsistent with a small, underfunded, unauthorized excavation.
- Never resolve a hidden consequence without an earlier, fair, visible signal having existed.

---

## What Writers Should Emphasize

- The physical and financial exhaustion underlying Daniel's experience.
- The specific, lived texture of Khuzestan as a real working region, not a backdrop.
- The procedural reality of excavation work, heritage risk, and financial pressure.
- The historical specificity of the Achaemenid caravan thread, grounded in real research.
- Small, concrete sensory and institutional details over broad historical exposition.

---

## Continuity Risks

- Drift toward confirming either side of the ambiguity across many scenes/writers without a single editorial check.
- Inconsistent geography or travel time between the excavation site, town, Susa, and industrial zones.
- Environmental hazards introduced in one scene being silently dropped as a risk in later scenes set in the same location.
- Local/secondary characters' economic and cultural texture drifting into generic stereotype over time as new writers join.
- Historical details introduced ad hoc by individual writers without basis in the package's research, contradicting previously established historical-layer facts.
